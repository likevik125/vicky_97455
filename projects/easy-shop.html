Easy Shop

An end-to-end e-commerce mobile app spanning 60+ high-fidelity screens.

Overview

Easy Shop is a comprehensive mobile shopping experience designed in a 3-month sprint with a 3-person agile team. The product covers discovery, product detail, filters, cart, checkout, order tracking, and account — over 60 production-ready screens driven by a cohesive visual system.

Problem

Existing shopping apps in the market overwhelmed users with dense catalogs, confusing filters, and checkout flows that dropped off before purchase. Small merchants needed a modern, mobile-first storefront that felt trustworthy and effortless.

Goals

  • Reduce checkout abandonment through frictionless flows.
  • Make search and filtering feel intuitive across large catalogs.
  • Ship a scalable design system usable across 60+ screens.
  • Deliver production-ready specs for engineering handoff.

Role

  • Owned end-to-end UX from research to high-fidelity delivery.
  • Ran discovery, defined personas, and mapped journeys.
  • Built the design system in Figma with tokens and components.
  • Produced interactive prototypes and specs for developers.
  • Designed marketing assets and LinkedIn carousels for launch.

Target users

Mobile-first shoppers aged 18–40 in Tier-1 and Tier-2 Indian cities who expect fast, trustworthy commerce and use their phone as the primary shopping device.

Research

Conducted 8 user interviews and audited 5 competing apps. Ran a lightweight card sort on category taxonomy and shadowed 3 users through a purchase to observe friction. Synthesised findings into personas, journey maps, and a prioritised opportunity list.

Personas

Priya, 28

Working professional

Needs

Quick reorders, saved addresses, transparent delivery windows.

Frustrations

Endless filters, unclear return policies, surprise charges at checkout.

Arjun, 34

Deal-driven shopper

Needs

Comparison, wishlist, price-drop alerts.

Frustrations

Hidden coupon codes, cluttered PDPs, forced sign-up.

Pain points

  • Filter panels were long, modal, and easy to lose progress in.
  • Checkout required too many steps and hid the final price too late.
  • Search returned irrelevant results without visible query controls.
  • Order tracking lived in email, not the app.

Information architecture

A four-tab shell (Home, Categories, Cart, Account) with progressive disclosure inside each. Search is persistent in the top bar; filters live in a bottom sheet so shoppers never lose the results grid.

Competitive analysis

Benchmarked against Amazon, Flipkart, Meesho, Myntra, and Ajio. Extracted patterns for filters, PDPs, and checkout while identifying gaps in trust cues, transparency, and clarity for first-time buyers.

Design decisions

  • Bottom-sheet filters preserve the result grid behind them.
  • Single-page checkout with inline validation replaced the 4-step flow.
  • Sticky total + delivery ETA visible from cart onwards.
  • Chips over dropdowns for variant selection on the PDP.

Wireframes

Low-fidelity flows validated navigation and information density before pixel work. Key screens — Home, Category, PDP, Cart, Checkout — were tested in grayscale with 5 users to isolate structural feedback from visual preference.

Visual design

A calm, product-forward aesthetic: generous whitespace, editorial typography, and full-bleed product imagery. A single warm accent guides the eye to primary actions without competing with merchandise.

Design system

Built a Figma library with color, type, spacing, and elevation tokens; 40+ reusable components (buttons, chips, inputs, cards, sheets); and documented states for every interactive element. Named tokens mirrored the developer contract for a 1:1 handoff.

Prototype

Assembled an interactive Figma prototype covering the full purchase journey, used in stakeholder reviews and a moderated usability round with 5 participants.

Accessibility

  • Type scale meets WCAG AA contrast on all backgrounds.
  • Tap targets sized 44px minimum across the system.
  • Form errors announced with icon + text, never colour alone.
  • Focus states specified for keyboard-first testing.

Developer handoff

Delivered a Figma library with dev-mode annotations, redlines for edge cases, and a component-to-code map. Held two handoff sessions with the engineering team to walk through motion, empty states, and error paths.

Challenges

  • Balancing catalogue density with clarity on small screens.
  • Designing filters that felt fast without hiding options.
  • Scaling a consistent system across 60+ screens with a small team.

Solutions

  • Modular card sizes and lazy-loading placeholders kept the grid legible.
  • Bottom-sheet filters with live result counts kept context visible.
  • A strict token system and component library removed drift.

Results

  • 60+ production-ready screens delivered on schedule.
  • Checkout compressed from 4 steps to a single scannable page.
  • Handoff completed with zero blocking design questions in week one.

Impact

The system gave the engineering team a single source of truth and gave stakeholders a shippable, coherent product story to fundraise around.

Key learnings

  • A tight token layer pays for itself by screen 20.
  • Users forgive density if hierarchy is honest.
  • Prototype early, prototype rough — polish later.

Future improvements

  • Personalised home feed driven by browse history.
  • In-app returns flow with pickup scheduling.
  • Loyalty layer with tier-based perks.
