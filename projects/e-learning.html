Interactive E-Learning Ecosystem

K-12 mathematics learning modules engineered for classrooms and self-study.

Overview

An interactive learning ecosystem for grades 10–12 mathematics. My work spanned building responsive, cross-browser modules, engineering complex equations with MathML, and translating instructional content into interactive, animated media.

Problem

Learners struggled to track progress and stay engaged across long courses with cluttered navigation, weak progress feedback, and a poor mobile experience — driving low completion.

Goals

  • Render complex mathematical content accurately across devices.
  • Improve completion by surfacing progress and structure clearly.
  • Ship pixel-perfect, accessible modules that meet WCAG standards.
  • Enable non-developers to add new lessons via a consistent template.

Role

  • Engineered lesson modules in HTML5, CSS3, and MathML.
  • Wrote lightweight JS to dynamically fetch and render assets.
  • Built animated instructional content in Adobe Animate CC.
  • Owned cross-browser QA and responsive behaviour.

Target users

Students aged 15–18 preparing for board exams, plus teachers running blended classrooms.

Research

Interviewed 12 learners across grades 10–12. Discovered weak progress feedback and unreliable mobile behaviour were the two biggest completion blockers. Ran informal reviews with two teachers to validate lesson pacing.

Pain points

  • Progress bars were cosmetic and reset on refresh.
  • Equations broke on smaller screens and older browsers.
  • Navigation between lessons hid the learner's place in the course.

Information architecture

Course → Chapter → Lesson → Concept. Every level exposes progress and the immediate next action so learners always know where they are.

Competitive analysis

Benchmarked Byju's, Vedantu, and Khan Academy. Adopted Khan-style incremental progress and lightweight navigation; avoided the heavy, ad-driven density of local competitors.

Design decisions

  • Progress persisted across sessions and surfaced at every level.
  • Equations rendered with MathML fallback to accessible SVG.
  • One primary action per lesson screen — no dead ends.

Wireframes

Wireframes prioritised the lesson canvas: content in the centre, progress on top, next action always visible below the fold.

Visual design

A calm, focused UI with consistent components, accessible colour, and a clear hierarchy tuned for long reading sessions.

Design system

A lesson-template pattern library covering headings, equations, callouts, illustrations, and inline practice — usable by non-designers to author new content.

Prototype

Interactive HTML prototypes were used for teacher reviews before wider rollout.

Accessibility

  • MathML for screen-reader-accessible equations.
  • Colour palette validated for AA contrast.
  • Keyboard-navigable practice questions.
  • Responsive layouts across phone, tablet, projector.

Developer handoff

Modules shipped as self-contained bundles with documented CSS variables so instructional designers could theme new courses without engineering support.

Challenges

  • Making advanced math legible on small screens.
  • Keeping module weight low for 4G learners.
  • Ensuring cross-browser parity on legacy school hardware.

Solutions

  • Fluid typography and responsive equation containers.
  • Lightweight, dependency-free JS and lazy-loaded media.
  • Progressive enhancement with tested fallbacks.

Results

  • Delivered pixel-perfect modules across grades 10–12.
  • Modules ran reliably across modern and legacy browsers.
  • Instructional team could ship new lessons without dev handoff.

Impact

Teachers reported smoother in-class demos and students could resume lessons without losing progress — the two loudest pieces of feedback from the initial audit.

Key learnings

  • In education, reliability beats novelty every time.
  • MathML repays the investment when accessibility matters.
  • A template system multiplies a small team's output.

Future improvements

  • Analytics dashboard for teachers to spot at-risk learners.
  • Adaptive practice that responds to answer patterns.
  • Offline-first PWA packaging.
